STRATEGIC MINERAL ASSESSMENT — INDIA 2025
COMPREHENSIVE NATIONAL CRITICAL MINERAL POSITION REVIEW
TOP SECRET — MISSION HEADQUARTERS

PREAMBLE

This assessment, mandated by the Cabinet Committee on Security (CCS) Resolution No. CCS/2024/CMM/
REV-003 dated 04 December 2024, provides a comprehensive review of India's position across the six 
priority critical minerals: lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese, graphite, and rare earth elements 
(REEs). The assessment is prepared annually by NCMM Mission HQ in coordination with the Geological 
Survey of India (GSI), KABIL, NMDC, and the Intelligence Bureau (IB) Minerals Desk. It informs the 
Union Budget's critical mineral allocation, India's bilateral mineral diplomacy posture, and the 
Cabinet's strategic reserve maintenance directives. This document synthesizes classified intelligence 
from multiple sources and must not be reproduced or distributed without express approval of the 
Secretary, Ministry of Mines.

SECTION 1: INDIA'S EV TRANSITION DEMAND IMPERATIVE

India's National EV Policy (2024) and updated FAME-III framework project:
  - 30% EV penetration for two-wheelers by FY2029-30
  - 25% for three-wheelers by FY2027-28
  - 20% for four-wheelers by FY2030-31
  - Full electrification of government fleet by FY2030-31 (52,000 vehicles)
  - 50 GWh battery manufacturing capacity by FY2029-30 (PLI-ACC target)

These targets translate to transformative mineral demand growth requiring urgent strategic action.

SECTION 2: MINERAL-BY-MINERAL STRATEGIC POSITION SUMMARY

2.1 LITHIUM

Domestic Resource: 5.9 million MT ore (Rajasthan, preliminary); 1.2 MT ore (J&K, revised downward)
Import Dependency: 100% (zero domestic production; Rajasthan production earliest FY2032)
Current Reserve Coverage: 8 months (against 18-month NCMM target)
Supply Risk Rating: CRITICAL (8.6/10)
Primary Suppliers: Australia (55%), Argentina (15%), Chile (5%), others
Threat: Australia export value-add policy; price volatility; Argentina political risk
Key Gap: 27,086 MT LCE shortfall vs. NCMM Phase-1 target (55.9% below)
Strategic Imperative: Emergency procurement programme; G2G agreement with Chile; KABIL equity in 
  Arafura (Australia); domestic refinery feasibility (Rajasthan/Gujarat)

2.2 COBALT

Domestic Resource: 2.1 million MT cobalt-bearing ore (Singhbhum, Jharkhand — inferred, CL2)
Import Dependency: 93% (7% domestic from copper co-production, HINDALCO/HCL)
Current Reserve Coverage: 6.2 months (against 12-month Phase-1 target)
Supply Risk Rating: CRITICAL (8.1/10)
Primary Suppliers: DRC (67%), Zambia (9%), Finland (8%), Philippines (6%), Australia (5%)
Threat: DRC political instability (M23 conflict, mineral export tax review); Katanga province security
Key Gap: 4,472 MT Co-eq shortfall vs. NCMM Phase-1 target (48.3% below)
Strategic Imperative: Diversify away from DRC; activate Zambia G2G MOU; Singhbhum mine expedite

2.3 NICKEL

Domestic Resource: Limited (laterite deposits in Odisha, ~0.1% Ni grade — sub-economic)
Import Dependency: 98% (negligible domestic production)
Current Reserve Coverage: 5.8 months (against 12-month Phase-1 target)
Supply Risk Rating: HIGH (7.4/10)
Primary Suppliers: Philippines (38%), Australia (24%), Indonesia (21%), Russia (via UAE, 17%)
Threat: Indonesia export ban on unprocessed ore (since 2020) — pressure shifting to HPAL; 
  Philippines supply continuity; Russia sanctions environment complicating OFAC compliance
Key Gap: 14,800 MT Ni-eq shortfall vs. 60,000 MT FY2025-26 target
Strategic Imperative: Accelerate Indonesia HPAL sourcing; BHP Australia long-term contract; 
  RINL ferronickel swap; NiSO₄ domestic processing at KABIL Vadodara

2.4 MANGANESE

Domestic Resource: Significant (India has 7th largest Mn reserves globally — Odisha, MP, Goa)
  MOIL reserves: 73 million MT Mn ore; current production 1.2 million MT/year
  Additional: Private mines (Tata Steel, Sandur Mn & Iron Ores Ltd.) contribute ~0.8 million MT/year
Import Dependency: 22% (for high-grade metallurgical Mn; domestic ore largely medium-grade)
Current Reserve Coverage: 18 months (above NCMM 12-month target — GREEN status)
Supply Risk Rating: LOW-MEDIUM (3.4/10)
Primary Import Sources: South Africa (45%), Australia (28%), Gabon (18%), Ghana (9%)
Threat: Manganese oversupply globally depressing MOIL prices; quality gap for battery-grade EMD
Key Opportunity: India is largely self-sufficient in Mn; opportunity to export to ASEAN markets
Strategic Imperative: Develop EMD (Electrolytic Manganese Dioxide) capacity for battery applications;
  MOIL-NCMM integrated strategy for battery-grade Mn sulfate production

2.5 GRAPHITE

Domestic Resource: India has significant crystalline graphite deposits (Rajasthan, AP, Odisha)
  GSI-estimated reserves: 154 million MT graphite ore, but mostly low to medium grade (3–8% C)
Import Dependency: For battery-grade: 100% (synthetic graphite) / 85% (natural spherical graphite)
Current Reserve Coverage: 4 months (critical for EV battery anode material — RED status)
Supply Risk Rating: VERY HIGH (9.1/10)
Primary Import Sources: China (72% of battery-grade natural graphite), Japan (19%), South Korea (9%)
Threat: China controls 65% of global natural graphite production and >80% of processing/coating;
  China October 2023 export restrictions on graphite (still partially in effect as of Q1 2025)
Key Gap: India has near-zero battery-grade graphite processing capacity domestically
Strategic Imperative: Emergency procurement from Mozambique (Syrah Resources), Japan (Mitsubishi/
  Showa Denko); pilot coating plant at NMDC or MECL; PLI scheme for anode material manufacturing

2.6 RARE EARTH ELEMENTS (REEs)

Domestic Resource: Monazite beach sands (world's 6th largest REE reserve — 6.9 million MT)
  IREL production: ~536 MT TREO/year (constrained by thorium regulations — Atomic Energy Act)
Import Dependency: 82% (China dominant; 65% of all imports are Chinese processed REEs)
Current Reserve Coverage: 3.5 months for critical magnet-grade REEs (Nd, Pr, Dy, Tb) — RED status
Supply Risk Rating: CRITICAL (9.3/10)
Primary Import Sources: China (82%), Australia (9%), India-IREL (4%), Brazil (2%), others (3%)
Threat: China's demonstrated REE supply weaponization capability; HREE extreme concentration risk
Key Gap: Zero domestic REE separation capacity at scale; IREL processing facility outdated
Strategic Imperative: IREL Vizag separation plant (3,000 MT TREO/year); Lynas India dedicated 
  processing stream; Arafura off-take; HREE strategic buffer emergency build-up

SECTION 3: SECURITY IMPLICATIONS FOR EV TRANSITION

India's EV transition is contingent on securing critical mineral supply chains that are currently 
dominated by geopolitical adversaries or at-risk partners. Key national security implications:

3.1 Defence Dependency Risk
  NdFeB magnets (requiring Nd, Pr, Dy, Tb) are essential for: 
  - BrahMos cruise missile guidance system motors
  - Akash SAM system radar servomotors  
  - Dhruv ALH helicopter motors
  - Arjun MBT electric turret drive system
  - INS Vikrant catapult system components (EMALS consideration)
  
  Current MoD REE stockpile: 14 months coverage (DRDO Strategic Reserve — classified location)
  MoD assessment: REE supply disruption would halt advanced weapons production within 6 months.

3.2 Energy Security Nexus
  India's 500 GW renewable energy target by 2030 requires approximately:
  - 180,000 MT NdPr (wind turbine permanent magnet generators)
  - 45,000 MT Dy (high-temperature magnet enhancer)
  - 1.2 million MT lithium carbonate (grid storage batteries)
  
  Without securing these minerals, India's energy security transition is fundamentally at risk.

3.3 Economic Security
  Downstream value addition in EV manufacturing (target: India as global EV hub by 2030) requires 
  reliable mineral inputs. Supply disruption could impact:
  - PLI-ACC investments of INR 18,100 Crore (already committed)
  - EV manufacturing sector projected employment: 5 million direct jobs by FY2030
  - Automobile export potential: USD 30 billion/year EV exports by FY2032

SECTION 5: TOP-LEVEL RECOMMENDATIONS TO CABINET COMMITTEE ON SECURITY

5.1 IMMEDIATE (Cabinet approval required within 30 days):
  (a) Authorize KABIL emergency procurement programme — INR 8,000 Crore (additional to budget)
      for lithium (15,000 MT LCE), cobalt (3,000 MT), REE-NdPr (2,000 MT), graphite (5,000 MT)
  (b) Classify graphite, REEs, and lithium as Hyper-Critical Minerals requiring 24-month reserve
  (c) Authorize KABIL equity acquisition in Arafura Rare Earths Australia (15% stake, est. AUD 280M)

5.2 SHORT-TERM (3–6 months):
  (a) Conclude G2G mineral agreements with Australia (upgrade), Chile, Tanzania, Zambia, and 
      Mozambique (target 5 new MoUs by September 2025)
  (b) Fast-track IREL Vizag separation plant — accelerate environmental clearance process
  (c) Launch NCMM Strategic Mineral Exchange (NSMEX) — internal government trading platform

5.3 MEDIUM-TERM (6–18 months):
  (a) Commission domestic lithium refinery feasibility (INR 50 Crore study)
  (b) Establish mineral processing SEZ at Kamarajar Port (Ennore) — battery materials focus
  (c) Create INR 25,000 Crore National Critical Mineral Fund (NCMF) through sovereign bond issuance
