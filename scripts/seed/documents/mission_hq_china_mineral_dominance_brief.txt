EXECUTIVE SUMMARY:
China exercises dominant control over the global critical mineral supply chain, posing a 
Category A Strategic Risk to India's energy transition, defence manufacturing, and 
semiconductor ambitions. This brief provides a classified assessment of China's 
processing dominance, strategic leverage mechanisms, and India's vulnerability 
exposure across seven critical mineral categories.

SECTION 1: CHINA'S PROCESSING MONOPOLY — CURRENT STATUS

1.1 Lithium
China controls approximately 65% of global lithium refining capacity. Despite holding 
only 6% of global lithium reserves, China has built 72% of global lithium chemical 
processing facilities. The Sichuan and Qinghai provinces host the world's largest 
lithium carbonate and lithium hydroxide production complexes.

India currently imports 100% of processed lithium carbonate and lithium hydroxide. 
Even Australia's Pilbara Minerals exports spodumene concentrate to China for processing 
before re-export. India has no domestic refining capacity as of FY2025.

1.2 Cobalt
China controls approximately 70% of global cobalt processing capacity. The DRC produces 
73% of mined cobalt globally, but 70% of this is shipped to China for processing. 
China's CATL and BYD have secured long-term offtake agreements covering DRC production 
through 2035. India sources cobalt hydroxide from DRC but depends on Chinese processed 
cobalt precursors for battery cathode manufacturing.

1.3 Rare Earth Elements (REE)
China's dominance in REE is near-total: 85% of global REE mining, 90% of REE processing, 
and 87% of NdFeB permanent magnet production. In 2023, China restricted exports of 
gallium, germanium, and graphite — precursors for semiconductors and EV batteries. 
India imports approximately 82% of its REE requirements from China or Chinese-controlled 
supply chains. NCMM has initiated bilateral agreements with Lynas (Australia), 
MP Materials (USA), and SRK Consulting (Canada) to diversify by 2027.

1.4 Natural Graphite
China produces 67% of global natural graphite and 98% of spherical graphite used in 
lithium-ion battery anodes. In October 2023, China imposed export licensing controls 
on graphite, creating immediate supply shocks for EV manufacturers globally.

1.5 Nickel
Indonesia (with heavy Chinese investment through IMIP and Nickel Industries) now 
controls 48% of global nickel production. Chinese-backed HPAL (High-Pressure Acid 
Leach) plants process Class 1 nickel required for batteries. Effectively, China controls 
the value chain even for Indonesian nickel.

1.6 Manganese
China controls 95% of electrolytic manganese metal (EMM) production and 60% of 
high-purity manganese sulphate (HPMS) for battery cathodes. India's domestic manganese 
ore production (Odisha, Maharashtra) covers only oxide grades — not battery-grade.

SECTION 2: STRATEGIC LEVERAGE MECHANISMS

2.1 Export Restriction Capability
China has demonstrated willingness to use mineral exports as geopolitical leverage:
- 2010: Rare earth embargo against Japan following Senkaku dispute
- 2023: Gallium, germanium, graphite export licensing
- 2024: Antimony export controls (critical for defence applications)
A similar action against India targeting cobalt precursors, rare earth magnets, or 
graphite anode materials would critically impair India's EV manufacturing within 90 days.

2.2 Price Manipulation
Chinese state-owned enterprises (SOEs) have demonstrated coordinated pricing to 
undercut non-Chinese producers, driving out Western processing capacity. This ensures 
dependency is maintained even when non-Chinese mines exist.

SECTION 3: INDIA'S VULNERABILITY ASSESSMENT

CRITICAL RISK INDICATORS (CRI Score: 8.2/10 — HIGH):
- Lithium carbonate: 100% import dependent, 0% domestic refining
- Cobalt: 100% import dependent, 0% domestic processing
- REE: 82% China-origin, no domestic separation facility
- Graphite anodes: 100% import dependent
- Strategic reserves coverage: 8.2 months (lithium), 26 months (cobalt), 18 months (manganese)

SECTION 4: RECOMMENDED MITIGATIONS (TOP SECRET)

4.1 IMMEDIATE (FY2025): Accelerate strategic reserve build-up to 24-month coverage
4.2 SHORT-TERM (FY2026): Establish India's first lithium refinery at Kutch Special 
    Economic Zone (capacity: 50,000 MT LCE per annum)
4.3 MEDIUM-TERM (FY2027): Operationalise Rajasthan lithium deposit (Degana-Nagaur)
4.4 STRATEGIC: Bilateral technology-for-access agreements with Argentina, Chile, 
    Australia, and Canada under the Critical Minerals Partnership framework
